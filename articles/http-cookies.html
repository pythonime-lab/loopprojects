What Is a Cookie?

An HTTP cookie (also called a web cookie or browser cookie) is a small text file that a website sends to your web browser. The browser may store cookies, create new cookies, modify existing ones, and send them back to the same server with later requests. These files typically contain small amounts of data about your activity on a website.

How Do Cookies Work?

The process is straightforward:

  • You visit a website - When you first visit a site, the web server generates a cookie.
  • The cookie is sent to your browser - The web server sends the cookie to the client (your browser), which stores it on your computer.
  • Your browser stores it - The cookie file is saved in your browser's storage.
  • It's sent back with future requests - The browser then sends cookies back to the server with every request, allowing the website to recognize you on return visits.

Why Do Websites Use Cookies?

Cookies serve three main purposes:

Session Management

Cookies provide secured interaction between users and app servers, handling things like logins, auto-fill features, and shopping carts. Without cookies, you'd have to log in again every time you clicked to a new page.

Personalization

Cookies remember your preferences. If a user logs out of a shopping website, their username can be stored in a cookie, and next time they load that website, it prompts them to log in with the username they used last time. Language settings, theme choices, and other preferences are stored this way.

Tracking

Some cookies record what websites users visit, and this information is sent to the server that originated the cookie the next time the browser has to load content from that server. This helps websites understand user behavior and can be used for analytics or advertising.

Types of Cookies

Session Cookies

These are deleted after a user's session ends—once they log out of their account on a website or exit the website. They're temporary and help manage your current browsing session.

Persistent Cookies

These remain in a user's browser for a predetermined length of time, which could be a day, a week, several months, or even years. They remember your preferences across multiple visits.

Third-Party Cookies

These are set by a different domain than the one the user is visiting and are typically loaded through embedded content like advertisements, social media widgets, or analytics scripts.

Key Facts About Cookies

  • Size: Browsers are generally limited to a maximum size per cookie, usually 4KB.
  • HTTP is Stateless: Without cookies, each retrieval of a web page would be an isolated event, largely unrelated to all other page views made by the user on the website. Cookies add memory to an otherwise memoryless protocol.
  • Browser Control: Most modern browsers allow you to view, manage, and delete cookies through their settings.

Privacy Considerations

Many users do not want their online behavior to be tracked, and users also lack visibility or control over what tracking services do with the data they collect. Some privacy laws, such as the EU's ePrivacy Directive, require websites to obtain user consent before using certain types of cookies.

Privacy Tip: Review your browser's privacy settings and consider using privacy modes or cookie management tools to control which cookies are stored and transmitted.
